Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy — longevity, anti-senescence, and healing explained.

Hyperbaric oxygen therapy means breathing oxygen at pressures higher than sea level. At increased atmospheric pressure, your lungs absorb more oxygen than normal. That oxygen gets carried deeper into tissue, accelerating the body's natural repair processes.

I was trained in hyperbaric medicine as a commercial diver — not because it was trendy, but because it was a clinical tool for treating decompression sickness and dive injuries. The same underlying science now has a growing body of research supporting its use for recovery, wellness, inflammation, and performance.

🫁

How it works

Pressurized air increases the partial pressure of oxygen in your lungs. Your plasma carries dissolved oxygen to areas with restricted blood flow — places that normal circulation can't easily reach.

🏠

Home vs. clinical

Clinical HBOT operates at 2–3 ATA with 100% oxygen under medical supervision. Home chambers typically run 1.3 ATA with ambient air or supplemental oxygen — milder, but still effective for wellness and recovery applications.

📋

Who it's for

Athletes seeking faster recovery, people managing chronic inflammation or fatigue, and health-conscious individuals who want a research-backed tool that's practical for daily use at home.

The Research

What the HBOT studies actually show — senescence, telomeres, healing, and performance.

I cite studies. I don't make medical claims. There's a difference, and it matters. Here's what the published literature has found — make of it what you will.

The foundation: U.S. Navy decompression sickness treatment protocols

The clinical basis for HBOT as a tissue repair and inflammation intervention was not established by wellness researchers — it was established by the U.S. Navy. Decompression sickness causes nitrogen bubble formation in tissue, producing localized inflammation, joint pain, and cellular injury. Recompression in a hyperbaric chamber forces nitrogen back into solution and floods damaged tissue with oxygen, resolving inflammation and accelerating repair. These protocols, developed over decades of military dive medicine research, are the same biological mechanism now being studied in longevity, recovery, and anti-aging applications. The U.S. Navy Dive Manual remains the foundational reference for hyperbaric oxygen as a clinical intervention.

U.S. Navy Diving Manual / Undersea and Hyperbaric Medical Society (UHMS)

Wound healing and tissue repair

Multiple randomized controlled trials have demonstrated that HBOT accelerates healing in chronic wounds, diabetic ulcers, and radiation-damaged tissue by increasing oxygen tension in hypoxic areas and stimulating angiogenesis. A Cochrane review of trials covering 147 diabetic foot ulcer patients showed a significant reduction in major amputation risk when HBOT was used as adjunctive therapy. Note: clinical wound healing studies use 2.0–2.4 ATA with 100% oxygen. Home chambers operate at 1.3 ATA with ambient air — a milder application of the same underlying mechanism.

Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews / PMC — NIH / Journal of the American College of Clinical Wound Specialists

Inflammation reduction

Under inflammatory conditions, HBOT has been shown to attenuate pro-inflammatory transcriptional signatures, reduce cytokine levels including IL-6 and TNF-α, and decrease markers of oxidative damage. Importantly, this anti-inflammatory effect is most pronounced in individuals who already have active inflammation — research in healthy volunteers without pre-existing inflammation shows minimal cytokine change. This is consistent with decompression sickness treatment: hyperbaric oxygen resolves inflammation in injured tissue specifically because the tissue is already in an inflammatory state. The mechanism is well established; the wellness application is an extension of that same biology.

Frontiers in Immunology (2026) / PMC — NIH / Scientific Reports (Nature, 2025)

Exercise-induced muscle injury and recovery

A 2025 meta-analysis of 10 studies covering 299 subjects found that HBOT significantly accelerated recovery from exercise-induced muscle injury across both higher and lower atmospheric pressures, with effects observed at both 60 and 100-minute session durations. It should be noted that the specific claim of HBOT reducing delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) in healthy individuals is not supported by the Cochrane evidence — that research was inconclusive. The recovery benefit applies to actual tissue injury and inflammatory states, consistent with the decompression sickness treatment model. Healthy muscle soreness without underlying injury is a different condition.

ScienceDirect — Systematic Review and Meta-analysis (2025) / Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews

Anti-senescence and telomere lengthening

A 2020 peer-reviewed study from the Sagol Center for Hyperbaric Medicine and Research, published in the journal Aging, found that 60 daily HBOT sessions in healthy adults aged 64 and older produced significant increases in telomere length across multiple cell types — over 20% on average — alongside reductions in senescent cell accumulation of up to 37%. The authors described it as the first demonstration of a non-pharmacological, clinically available method for senescent cell clearance. Protocol used: 100% oxygen at 2 ATA for 90 minutes per session. Home chambers operate at 1.3 ATA with ambient air — this is a milder application, and home-specific equivalence data is still emerging.

Aging (Albany NY) — Hachmo Y, Hadanny A, et al., 2020 · doi:10.18632/aging.202188 · PubMed PMID: 33206062

Cognitive performance and neuroplasticity in aging populations

Research from the Sagol Center has examined HBOT's effect on age-related cognitive decline, showing improvements in attention, processing speed, and executive function in older adults alongside measurable increases in cerebral blood flow. The mechanism — increased oxygen delivery to hypoxic tissue — mirrors the same principle applied in dive medicine for hypoxic injury treatment.

Aging (Albany NY) / GeroScience / Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience
